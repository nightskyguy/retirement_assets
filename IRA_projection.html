IRA Projection Tool — How to Use

What is this?

A visual model for understanding how a pre-tax IRA grows or depletes over time — and what that means for your taxes. It shows how Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) can force income you may not need, and lets you explore different futures by adjusting growth rates, withdrawal rates, and outside income sources.

This tool was built specifically to model large IRA balances and the cascading RMD effects that can follow unexpected growth. Once an IRA grows large enough, RMDs can push you into higher tax brackets, trigger IRMAA surcharges, and — for a surviving spouse filing single — cause a severe "Widow's Tax" cliff. This tool makes those dynamics visible.

What can I explore?

  • Withdrawal rate vs. balance longevity — Dial your base withdrawal and observe whether the balance survives through your projection window, and at what point RMDs take over.
  • Growth rate sensitivity — Try higher and lower growth rates to see how dramatically a large IRA can overshoot (or undershoot) expectations.
  • Roth conversion headroom — Set additional income to reflect SS and other sources. Then raise your withdrawal above your lifestyle needs. The gap between the withdrawal line and the next bracket ceiling is roughly how much you could convert to Roth each year — and the balance chart shows whether that pace is sustainable.
  • MFJ vs. Single ("Widow's Tax") — Toggle the filing-status switch. The bracket thresholds roughly halve when filing single, often pushing the same income into a dramatically higher bracket and triggering IRMAA tiers that were previously clear.
  • Inflation's toll — Switch to Today's $ mode to see the gap between the nominal IRA balance and its real purchasing power. The blue real-balance line is the one that actually matters for spending.

Left Panel — Inputs

  • Current age / Withdrawal start age — projection begins at current age; voluntary withdrawals start at the indicated age (must be ≥ current age).
  • Starting balance / Annual growth — today's pre-tax IRA balance and expected nominal annual return.
  • Base withdrawal — annual spend in today's dollars; grown with inflation each projection year.
  • Display in Future $ / Today's $ — toggles whether right-axis lines (withdrawals, thresholds) are shown in nominal or inflation-deflated terms. Balance lines always show both.
  • Infl-adj add'l income — income that grows with inflation: Social Security (use 85% of gross benefit), TIPS, etc.
  • Fixed add'l income — income fixed in nominal terms: pension, interest, dividends, capital gains.

Both income fields shift threshold lines down, showing only the IRA-withdrawal headroom remaining within each bracket after accounting for other income.

Withdrawal Logic

  • Each year: balance grows → RMD calculated (age ≥ 75, IRS Uniform Lifetime Table) → actual withdrawal = max(inflation-adj base, RMD).
  • No withdrawal before the withdrawal start age, even if RMD age has been reached.
  • SECURE 2.0: RMDs start at 75 for those born after 1958.

Tax & IRMAA Threshold Lines

  • Colored bands — federal income tax brackets. Each filled band represents one bracket's income range. Bracket lines can be toggled off in the legend; bands always extend to the next bracket regardless.
  • Red lines — Medicare IRMAA surcharge tiers. These are cliffs: one dollar over the line triggers the full annual surcharge. No fill — the penalty is not marginal.
  • Thresholds are 2026 estimates (std deduction + bracket floor), grown with inflation. Offset down by any additional income you enter.
  • Click any legend item to show or hide it. All threshold rows also sort low → high automatically.

Metric Cards

  • Nominal / Real bal — IRA balance at end of projection in future and today's dollars.
  • Peak RMD — largest required minimum distribution over the projection period.
  • Underfunded at — first year the IRA balance falls below that year's inflation-adjusted withdrawal target.

Limitations — How this differs from reality

  • Growth is constant here; markets are not. A single annual rate is a useful fiction. Actual returns fluctuate with market conditions, your investment mix, and sequence-of-returns risk.
  • Tax law changes. Brackets, standard deductions, and IRMAA thresholds are all subject to legislation. Future Medicare costs and IRMAA surcharges are likely to grow, further eroding net spendable income over time.
  • Net spendable income is not calculated. The tool shows gross income and bracket placement, not after-tax cash in hand.
  • No state taxes. Most states tax IRA withdrawals; some exempt Social Security. State brackets can meaningfully compound federal rates.
  • No external assets. Brokerage accounts, savings, real estate, and other investments are not modeled. Spending from outside the IRA changes the picture substantially.
  • Fixed withdrawal is unrealistic. Actual spending changes with health, lifestyle, and life events. This model assumes you hold to the same inflation-adjusted base.
  • Longevity is unknown. Neither your lifespan nor your spouse's is factored in. Plan accordingly.
  • Income timing matters. There is no way to model starting Social Security at different ages for two spouses, or income that phases in or out over time.

Tips

  • Amber rows in the table = RMD exceeds your target withdrawal. Red rows = depleted balance.
  • For IRMAA planning: qualified charitable distributions (QCDs) do not count toward MAGI.
  • Try Today's $ mode to see whether your real purchasing power holds steady over time.
